    Section 3.2 Disk Method

    Exercise 23. Cross-Sectional Volume Formula.

    A solid is oriented along the \(x\)-axis from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\text{,}\) and its cross-sectional area at position \(x\) is \(A(x)\text{.}\) Which integral gives the volume of the solid?
    🔗
    • \(\displaystyle\int_a^b A(x)\, dx\)
    • The volume of a thin slice at position \(x\) is approximately \(A(x)\,\Delta x\) (area times thickness). Summing these slices and taking a limit gives the definite integral \(\int_a^b A(x)\, dx\text{.}\)
    • \(\displaystyle\int_a^b A(x)^2\, dx\)
    • We do not square the cross-sectional area. Each slice has volume \(A(x)\,\Delta x\text{,}\) not \(A(x)^2\,\Delta x\text{.}\) Squaring the radius (not the area) appears in the Disk Method, which is a specific case.
    • \(\displaystyle\int_a^b \pi\, A(x)\, dx\)
    • The factor of \(\pi\) is not needed in the general formula. It only appears when the cross section is a circle (as in the Disk Method), because the area of a circle of radius \(R\) is \(\pi R^2\text{.}\)
    • \(\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}\big[A(x)\big]\)
    • The derivative of the area function does not give volume. Volume is found by integrating cross-sectional area over the length of the solid.

    Exercise 24. Disk Method Radius Identification.

    The curve \(y = \sqrt{x}\) is revolved about the \(x\)-axis from \(x=0\) to \(x=4\text{.}\) Using the Disk Method, what is the radius function \(R(x)\) and the resulting volume integral?
    🔗
    • \(R(x)=\sqrt{x}\text{,}\) so \(\displaystyle V = \pi\int_0^4 x\, dx\)
    • The radius of each disk is the distance from the \(x\)-axis to the curve, which is \(R(x)=\sqrt{x}\text{.}\) Squaring gives \(R(x)^2 = x\text{,}\) so the volume is \(\pi\int_0^4 x\, dx\text{.}\)
    • \(R(x)=x\text{,}\) so \(\displaystyle V = \pi\int_0^4 x^2\, dx\)
    • The radius is the \(y\)-value of the curve at each \(x\text{,}\) which is \(\sqrt{x}\text{,}\) not \(x\text{.}\) The function \(y=\sqrt{x}\) tells us the distance from the axis of rotation to the curve.
    • \(R(x)=\sqrt{x}\text{,}\) so \(\displaystyle V = \pi\int_0^4 \sqrt{x}\, dx\)
    • The radius is correct, but remember the Disk Method formula uses \(R(x)^2\text{,}\) not \(R(x)\text{.}\) So we need \((\sqrt{x})^2 = x\) inside the integral.
    • \(R(x)=x^2\text{,}\) so \(\displaystyle V = \pi\int_0^4 x^4\, dx\)
    • The curve is \(y = \sqrt{x}\text{,}\) not \(y = x^2\text{.}\) The radius equals the \(y\)-value of the curve being revolved.

    Exercise 25. Disk Method vs. Washer Method.

    A student is setting up a volume integral for a solid of revolution. Which condition determines whether the disk method should be used instead of the washer method?
    🔗
    • When one boundary of the region being rotated lies on the axis of rotation.
    • Correct! When one edge of the region coincides with the axis of rotation, the inner radius is zero, the cross-sectional "washer" collapses to a solid disk, and the volume formula simplifies to \(V = \pi \int_a^b [f(x)]^2 \, dx\text{.}\) As soon as a gap opens between the region and the axis, both an outer radius \(R\) and an inner radius \(r\) are present and the washer formula \(V = \pi \int_a^b \bigl([R(x)]^2 - [r(x)]^2\bigr) dx\) is required.
    • When the region is rotated about a horizontal axis.
    • Not quite. Both the disk method and the washer method work for rotation about either a horizontal or a vertical axis. The orientation of the axis determines whether you integrate with respect to \(x\) or \(y\text{,}\) but it does not determine which method to use. The deciding factor is whether a gap exists between the region and the axis.
    • When the region is bounded by a single function.
    • This is a common mix-up. In both methods the region is typically bounded by two curves (or a curve and a boundary line). What distinguishes the methods is not the number of bounding functions, but whether one of those boundaries is the axis of rotation itself. If it is, the inner radius vanishes and you use the disk method; if neither boundary coincides with the axis, you need the washer method.
    • When the boundaries of the region are given by functions of \(x\).
    • The method does not depend on whether the boundaries are functions of \(x\) or \(y\text{.}\) Both methods can be applied in either case; the key is whether one of those boundaries coincides with the axis of rotation.
    • When the boundaries of a region intersect at one point.
    • The number of intersection points does not determine which method to use. Even if the boundaries intersect at only one point, they may still both lie above the axis of rotation, creating a gap that requires the washer method. Conversely, if they intersect at one point but one boundary coincides with the axis, the disk method applies.

    Exercise 29. Washer Method: Identify the Error.

    The region bounded by \(y = \sqrt{x}\) and \(y = x\) for \(0 \le x \le 1\) is revolved about the line \(y = -1\text{.}\) A student sets up the following volume integral using the washer method:
    \begin{equation*} V = \pi\int_0^1\Bigl[(\sqrt{x})^2 - x^2\Bigr]\,dx = \pi\int_0^1\bigl(x - x^2\bigr)\,dx. \end{equation*}
    Which statement best identifies the error in the student’s work?
    🔗
    • The radii were measured from \(y = 0\) instead of from the axis \(y = -1\text{.}\)
    • Correct! Because the axis \(y=-1\) lies below the region, each radius must be increased by \(1\) to account for the gap between the axis and the nearest boundary. In general, when the axis is \(y = c\text{,}\) the radii are \(R(x) = f(x) - c\) and \(r(x) = g(x) - c\text{.}\)
    • The limits of integration should run from \(-1\) to \(1\) to reflect the position of the axis.
    • Incorrect. The limits of integration are determined by the x-extent of the region being rotated, which is still \([0,1]\text{.}\) Shifting the axis of rotation never changes the limits; it changes the radius expressions instead.
    • The outer and inner radii are swapped; it should be \(R(x) = x\) and \(r(x) = \sqrt{x}\) since \(y = x \ge y = \sqrt{x}\) on \([0,1]\text{.}\)
    • Incorrect. On \([0,1]\) we have \(\sqrt{x} \ge x\) (check: at \(x = \tfrac{1}{4}\text{,}\) \(\sqrt{x} = \tfrac{1}{2} > \tfrac{1}{4} = x\)), so \(y = \sqrt{x}\) is indeed the outer boundary. The student has the orientation right but is measuring radii from the wrong baseline.
    • The formula should use \(2\pi\) instead of \(\pi\) because the solid is generated by a full \(360°\) rotation.
    • Incorrect. The washer-method formula is \(V = \pi\int(R^2-r^2)\,dx\text{;}\) the factor of \(\pi\) (not \(2\pi\)) already accounts for a full revolution. The factor \(2\pi\) appears in the shell method, where it represents the circumference of a cylindrical shell.

    Exercise 31. Washer Method: Select All Correct Volume Integrals.

    The region bounded by \(y = x^2\) and \(y = 1\) for \(0 \le x \le 1\) is revolved about the \(x\)-axis. Select every integral below that correctly computes the volume of the resulting solid.
    🔗
    • \(V = \pi\displaystyle\int_0^1\bigl(1 - x^4\bigr)\,dx\)
    • Correct. Using the washer method with vertical slices: the outer radius is \(R(x) = 1\) and the inner radius is \(r(x) = x^2\text{,}\) so \(R^2 - r^2 = 1 - x^4\text{.}\)
    • \(V = 2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^1 y\sqrt{y}\,dy\)
    • Correct. Using the shell method with horizontal shells (integrating in \(y\)): the shell at height \(y\) has radius \(y\) (distance to the \(x\)-axis) and length \(\sqrt{y}\) (from \(x=0\) to \(x=\sqrt{y}\)), giving \(2\pi\int_0^1 y^{3/2}\,dy = \tfrac{4\pi}{5}\text{,}\) which equals the washer-method result.
    • \(V = \pi\displaystyle\int_0^1\bigl(1-x^2\bigr)^2\,dx\)
    • Incorrect. This computes \(\pi\int(R - r)^2\,dx\text{,}\) which is not the washer formula. The correct integrand is \(R^2 - r^2\text{,}\) not \((R - r)^2\text{.}\) These are equal only when one radius is zero.
    • \(V = \pi\displaystyle\int_0^1 x^4\,dx\)
    • Incorrect. This integral gives the volume of the inner solid alone (the region below \(y = x^2\) only). The volume of the full washer-shaped solid requires subtracting the inner volume from the outer, i.e., \(\pi\int_0^1(1 - x^4)\,dx\text{.}\)
    • \(V = 2\pi\displaystyle\int_0^1\sqrt{y}\,dy\)
    • Incorrect. This is missing the radius factor \(y\) inside the integral. In the shell method, both the radius and the height of each shell must appear: the integrand should be \(y \cdot \sqrt{y} = y^{3/2}\text{,}\) not just \(\sqrt{y}\text{.}\)
